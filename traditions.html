There are many traditions we have but some are more important and funnier than others. I have highlighted the ones necessary in order to have a “true Schmitt Christmas.”

Coordinating or matching outfits



My parents enjoyed making us suffer and keeping photographic evidence of it. Throughout the late 80s-early 90s, we often had Christmas-themed outfits that coordinated well together. My little sister and I were often in matching dresses while my brothers wore complementing colors (except the year they wore matching lederhosen, which has become a favorite running joke we bring out every Christmas. They always made sure that we all looked good together, whether it be in matching outfits or in color-coordinating outfits. Now we try to make sure that we don't wear too many similar outfits (in the last few years, we have all verified what we were wearing ahead of time to avoid matching).


Someone to ruin Christmas

It is really not officially Christmas at my house until it has been declared ruined. Confession: I am usually the person who “wins” this honor. It is usually awarded after I do something totally ridiculous or unintentionally funny such as leaving all the price tags on everyone's presents when I wrap them (this happened once when I was 12 and I still hear about it). It all started one year when I went on a rant about my hatred for basically everything on the Christmas dinner menu (see food for more on this) and my mom specifically said “Kate-you just RUINED Christmas.” After that it became a running joke to see how I could “ruin” Christmas every year and if anyone else did something worthy of it, they were given the title (unfortunately this has only happened two other times). Luckily for me, my brother's fianc√©e, as the newest guest, was given the honor last year (for deciding to take a nap and dressing their dog up in a Santa suit) and will probably win again this year since my brothers like to tease her.


Complaining about Mass

We have gone to Christmas Eve 4:30/5:00pm mass for as long as I can remember. Part of this tradition involves my mother panicking that we are going to be too “late” to get seats. “Late” usually means we are at our house at 3:15(we live 5 minutes away). My brothers and I have fought this for years and once my oldest brother turned 16, began to drive on our own. Every year, my mom begrudgingly accepts this but always states, “this year I'm not saving you seats, so if you have to stand you only have yourselves to blame!” and every year when we show up at 4:00 (sometimes after a text/call from my dad), our seats are still there and we still get stuck sitting around (my dad enforces an iphones ban in church) waiting for mass to begin because it always starts late.


Playing some version of Trivial Pursuit & Arguing

Christmas night is when one of the more important traditions occurs at the Schmitt house. While regular board games have been banned for awhile (there have been a few unfortunate instances of games ending in tears or fistfights), Trivial Pursuit™ has become our go-to game since “it requires actual knowledge and skills unlike Monopoly™,” as my dad once stated. We own several versions (from the original 1980s version to the 90s edition) and try to switch it up every year. We generally play at night and the game can take a few hours depending on the version, but it almost always involves some sort of dispute. The now infamous game of 2009 involved my mom getting tired of the game and letting my oldest brother answer a question wrong to win, much to my other brother and my anger (“the tunnel but it's clever or something” is not the same thing as “The Chunnel,” no matter what he claims). The game is also marked by everyone knowing the answer to every question but the ones they were asked so there is a lot of ribbing if you get your question wrong since everyone knows the answer to it. There is quite a bit of trash–talking as well since it is a point of pride to be the “Christmas Trivial Pursuit™ Champion” since the title lasts for a year and can be used to bolster any argument occuring throughout the next year. No Christmas at the Schmitts is complete without some friendly competition over who knows the most useless trivia.
